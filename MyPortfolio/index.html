Project 1: Smartphone Interaction Workaround

Sketch, design, implement and document via a portfolio an animated touch-based and sensor based interface for a mobile phone that is an alternative to ‘slide to unlock’.
As a group of five, we firstly sketched ten original sketches on our own and then discussed together and picked roughly 5 from all of our ideas. Next, we present the refinement sketches to the class and received some critiques. Finally, we made the unlock app using Android Studio.

Sensor-Based

The sensor-based unlock interface uses the light sensor to control the eyes opening and closing animation on the screen. When using fingers to cover the light sensor, the lux value decrements to a certain range, and the eyes will close. The pupils will move following the movement of the phone. After the eyes' movement and blink, the screen will then unlock. As an additional feature, you can change the eye styles by shaking the phone.
This interface can avoid accidental unlock since the user need to follow a certain eye movement pattern (e.g., in our demo, left-most to right-most and close) to unlock.

Touch-Based

The touch-based unlock interface is a variation of the "Where's Waldo" game. Different Waldo pictures are placed on the photos from the user's photo gallery. The user need to click all the Waldo'es on the screen to successfully unlock the phone.
This interface can avoid accidental unlock since the user need to click a series positions on the screen (e.g., where the Waldo is) to unlock.

Project 2: Solve an Everyday Problem with Arduino


Create a physical prototype with Arduino to solve an everyday problem. The prototype must have at least one output and input device, and all wires/boards must be hidden by using physical prototyping.

Following the same steps as the previous projects, our group first came up with various ideas and went through the critique in class. At the end, we decided to choose Jason's key hook idea because it relates most to our daily life and was favoured by most of our classmates.

Problem Description
The issue we wanted to address is that for those who always need to carry the keys, they may sometimes forget where they put the keys, or they may forget to take out their keychain from the pocket so it would potentially be washed. Even though there are key hooks that help people to solve this problem, users may still forget due to carelessness. Below is a persona for describing the audience for our product.

Xi - "A Careless Student Living on Campus"

Take myself an as an example of the target user. I am an international student living in a four-room apartment of the university residence. I have to bring my key with me everyday unless I wish to bother the campus security or my roommates who nobody knows are at where. But I am such a forgetful person that sometimes creates trouble to myself and others.
My key is an electric key for opening the apartment door, and I also have many other stuffs attached to my keychain (e.g. membership card, usb drive, and a cute Stitch) which cannot be thrown into a washer or a dryer. The apartment does have a coat hook where I can put my keychain on, but it is almost one head taller than me and is nailed to the wall, which is inconvenient to use.

Final Look and Design Decisions


We covered all the wires and boards in a black wood box. The hooks are made from Copper wires. There are green and red LEDs on top of each hook indicating whether there's a key on the hook. On the left side of the box is a button for resetting the system if the alarm was triggered accidentally. On the right side is a PIR motion sensor. The buzzer is placed at the bottom of the box.

We use PIR motion sensor to detect the door open activity. Another input "sensor" is the hand made Copper wire. We have considered many ways to detect key attachment including the range sensor and weight sensor but finalized to Jason's Copper idea because this is simple and error free. A pair of Copper wires connect to an input pin and the ground, so when a keychain is hooked, there will be a circuit, and the digital pin state will change from HIGH to LOW. The button was added as we were refining our design to improve the robustness of the product.

The outputs consists of three pair of LED lights and a buzzer. As our system will have an expire time from last triggered alarm, and the PIR sensor is disabled in this period, so I added some lights features to indicate the system status. The LED lights will flash from left to right when a key is hooked or taken and flash right to left when the system was reset to initial mode. I also encoded a Super Mario melody for the buzzer to make the alarm interesting and pleasing.
